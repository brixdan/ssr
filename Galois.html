Algebraic extension of F is an extension via roots of some polynomial of n-th order: E = F[\( \alpha_1...\alpha_n \)]. Suppose that E is an extension of the field F (written as E/F and read "E over F"). An automorphism of E/F is defined to be an automorphism of E that fixes F pointwise. In other words, an automorphism of E/F is an isomorphism α from E to E such that α(x) = x for each x ∈ F. The set of all automorphisms of E/F forms a group with the operation of function composition. This group is sometimes denoted by Aut(E/F). If E/F is a Galois extension (E is a splitting field of a separable polynomial with coefficients in F), then Aut(E/F) is called the "Galois group of (the extension) E over F, and is usually denoted by Gal(E/F). If E/F is not a Galois extension, then the Galois group of (the extension) E over F is sometimes defined as Aut(G/F), where G is the Galois closure of E.
As an illustration, consider \(f (x) = (x^2 − 2)(x^2 − 3) \text{ in } Q[x] \), which has the roots \( \alpha_1, \alpha_2 = \pm \sqrt {2} \) and \( \alpha_3, \alpha_4 = \pm \sqrt {3} \). There are numerous polynomials q in \( Q[x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4] \) such that \( q( \alpha_1,\alpha_2,\alpha_3,\alpha_4 ) = 0 \), for example \begin{equation} q(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4) = x_3^2 - x_1^2 - 1 = 0 \end{equation} Formal substitution of roots \( (\alpha_1,\alpha_3) \) makes this form invalid. So \( S_4 \) is too big for a group of Automorphisms of this particular field.

Idea behind

Lots of heavy and complicated books are written to get insights into this whole thing of Abel-Ruffini theorem that quintic equations cannot be solved by radicals. But from point of view of multiverse (virtuality) it becomes a very simple idea. Every abstract finite group can be digitised (or mounted, or substantiated) into subgroup of some \( S_n \) space. We say "space" because it splits into dimentions. But they are not equal since many different ways of digitising exsist. Abstract group lives in multiverse, \( S_n \) - in our concrete space time. Nevertheless, abstract group is consistent, without contradictions and can be digitised at any time.
When abstract group has a property, all it's digit instances have that same property, independently of the way of digitising. Suppose, field E, subfield of a finitely generated field G, is an algebraic extension of field F: E = F[\( \alpha \)]. Then, \( Aut_E(G) \), group of automorphisms of field G that fixes E pointwise \( ( \forall \varphi \in Aut_E(G) \text { and } \forall e \in E \text {, } \varphi(e) = e) \) is apparently finite group, as is \( Aut_F(G) \). And \( Aut_E(G) \) is a subgroup of \( Aut_F(G) \), which may well appear to be proper. This property of being abstract subgroup, fixing extension field is independent of digitising method (roots renaming) and thus equivalent to being normal subgroup.

Suppose there be an equation \begin{equation} a_0x^n + a_1x^{n-1} + ... + a_n = 0 \text{, where } a_i \in Q \end{equation} which has n distinct roots \( \alpha_1,...,\alpha_n \) not in Q, then:

  • Those roots form a basis of field \( E = Q[\alpha_1,...,\alpha_n] \) in a sense that every element of E is a quotient of it's polynomials of degrees less then n.
  • Every automorphism \( Aut_Q(E) \) preserves any identity in E. In particular, equation (5). That is why it must trasfer any root \( \alpha_i \) to some other (or the same) root \( \alpha_j \), because there are no other roots of (5) except those in \( \{ \alpha_1,...,\alpha_n \} \)
  • Any permutation of roots \( \{ \alpha_1,...,\alpha_n \} \) being the basis of E induces exactly one automorphism \( Aut_Q(E) \) which means there are exactly n! of them (number of permutations in \( S_n \))

Suppose there be a formula for general roots of (5) in radicals. It must contain a few innermost radicals (that doesn't contain inner radicals). By taking sequential extensions by one of them, one could produce a chain of extensions ... \( Q[\sqrt ] \)... and a corresponding chain of subgroups in the group \( Aut_Q(E) \) fixing those extensions instead of Q. Apparently, they are subgroups. Suppose, there's no proper subgroup in all of that extensions chain. Then, at some step subgroup must jump from G to {e}. Radical, producing such an extension, abides, apparently, equation \( x^k = b \in E_i \) - where \( E_i \) - some previous extension field, for some k < n. Then we have a jump by cyclic extension (some minor details omitted for case when k is not a prime) from the whole group G = \( Aut_Q(E) \) to identity group \( \{ e \} \) and thus all G must be cyclic, which is a contradiction. Then, there must be a proper subgroup N in the \( Aut_Q(E) \) fixing elements of some subfield of E. But that property is abstract, independent of roots enumeration, which translates in being normal property of a subgroup: \begin{equation} \forall g \in G, \forall n \in N \text { } gng^{-1} \in N \end{equation}

So, the simple idea of abstract property of pointwise fixing being translated in normality property in \( S_n \) is all that is needed for the solution of the problem. Which illustrates the difference of being virtual and being concrete. Complexity of equations space grows rapidly with dimensions. Single dimension is a school program of math (quadratic equation). Two dimensional space (cubic equations) is for specialists (Cardano, Omar Khayyam). Three dimensional space requires exceptional minds like Abel, Ruffini, Galois. Four dimensional space is impossible by complexity and, as we suspect, by physical reasons.
